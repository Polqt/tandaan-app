---
title: Why narrative replays beat static changelogs
description: Teams need sequence, rationale, and chapter context, not only diffs.
---

Static changelogs tell you **what** changed. Narrative replays explain **how and why** it changed.

The difference matters more than you think. A changelog entry like *"Updated pricing section"* tells a reviewer nothing about the reasoning, the alternatives considered, or who drove the decision. A narrative replay lets them watch the edit unfold, chapter by chapter, with rationale tags attached to every meaningful moment.

## The problem with diffs

Most collaboration tools treat history as a technical artifact. You get a list of insertions and deletions, maybe a timestamp and an author name. But real product work is messier:

- A section gets rewritten three times before the team settles on the right framing.
- Two people edit the same paragraph simultaneously, and the merge creates something neither intended.
- A policy decision happens in a Slack thread, and the resulting document change has no trace of the context.

Diffs capture the *output* of these moments. Replays capture the *story*.

## Key advantages

### 1. Faster onboarding to project context

New team members can watch a 6-minute replay and understand not just the current state of a document, but the reasoning behind its structure. This replaces hours of "can you walk me through this?" meetings.

### 2. Better async review quality

When a reviewer can see the editing sequence — the false starts, the pivots, the final convergence — they give better feedback. They understand what was already tried and why it was abandoned.

### 3. Sharper product handoffs

Product managers hand off specs to engineers all the time. A spec with a narrative replay attached answers the inevitable "why did you choose this approach?" questions before they are even asked.

## When to use replays

Narrative replay becomes especially useful for cross-functional teams where decisions happen over multiple sessions. If your team spans time zones, or if you frequently onboard new people mid-project, replays turn your documents into self-explaining artifacts.

> The best documentation is documentation that explains itself. Narrative replays are the closest we have gotten to that ideal.

Try it yourself — create a document in Tandaan, write for a few sessions, mark some chapter points, and share the replay with a colleague. The reaction is usually the same: *"Why doesn't every tool do this?"*
