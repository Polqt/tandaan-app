---
title: Designing trust traces for async teams
description: Provenance metadata creates confidence in shared replay artifacts.
---

Trust traces attach provenance directly to replay frames. Every chapter in a Tandaan replay carries metadata about **who** made the change, **when** it happened, **what** sources informed the decision, and **why** the author chose that direction.

This is not just transparency theater. It is a structural solution to a real problem: async teams struggle to trust artifacts they did not witness being created.

## The trust gap in async work

When you are in a meeting and watch someone make an edit, you trust the result because you saw the reasoning in real time. But when you receive a document update overnight from a teammate in a different time zone, you are left guessing:

- Was this change based on customer feedback or personal preference?
- Did anyone else review this before it landed?
- Is the edit consistent with the direction we agreed on last week?

Without context, reviewers default to suspicion or rubber-stamping. Neither is good.

## Recommended metadata

Every trust trace in Tandaan captures four dimensions:

| Metadata | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Author ID** | Clerk-authenticated identity of the person who made the edit |
| **Timestamp** | Exact time the change was captured, synced across participants |
| **Source references** | Links to Slack threads, Figma files, customer calls, or any external context that informed the decision |
| **Decision note** | A short, optional annotation explaining the rationale (e.g., "Aligned with Q2 roadmap priorities") |

## How trust traces work in practice

### During editing

As you write in Tandaan, the system automatically captures author ID and timestamp. Source references and decision notes are optional — you add them when a change is significant enough to warrant explanation.

### During review

When a reviewer watches a replay, trust trace metadata surfaces inline. They can see who wrote each section, when it was written, and what informed it. If a decision note exists, it appears as a contextual annotation on the chapter.

### During audit

For compliance-sensitive teams, trust traces create an audit trail that goes beyond "who changed what." They capture the *reasoning* behind changes, which is what auditors actually care about.

## Design principles

We followed three principles when designing trust traces:

1. **Low friction capture.** If adding context is harder than skipping it, people will skip it. Author and timestamp are automatic. Source links and notes are one-click additions.
2. **Inline, not separate.** Trust metadata lives on the replay frame, not in a separate audit log. Context should appear where you need it, not in a different tool.
3. **Verifiable, not decorative.** Author identities are Clerk-authenticated. Timestamps are server-generated. Source links are preserved as snapshots.

## The result

When teams can verify context, async collaboration becomes less ambiguous and much faster. Reviewers spend less time asking "why?" and more time building on what exists. Trust compounds — and trust traces are the mechanism that makes it compound across time zones and team changes.
